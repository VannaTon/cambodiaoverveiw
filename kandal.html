WELCOME TO KALDAL PROVINCE



GENERAL

Kandal Province, an essential region in Cambodia’s heartland, wraps around the bustling capital of Phnom Penh like a protective embrace, serving as both a gateway and a retreat from the urban sprawl. The province is characterized by its lush, fertile landscape, nurtured by the life-giving waters of the Mekong and Tonle Sap rivers that weave through it. These rivers are the lifeblood of the province, supporting a thriving agricultural economy. Fields of emerald-green rice stretch as far as the eye can see, interspersed with orchards laden with tropical fruits such as mangoes, bananas, and durians, which are central to the local diet and economy.


Kandal’s rich history is deeply rooted in its ancient temples and monuments. One of the most significant historical sites is Oudong, the former royal capital of Cambodia. Perched on a series of hills, Oudong was the center of the kingdom from the 17th to the 19th centuries and remains a place of immense cultural and spiritual importance. The site is adorned with several grand stupas, each containing relics of former kings, and it offers breathtaking views of the surrounding plains. Pilgrims and tourists alike are drawn to Oudong not only for its historical significance but also for the serene and contemplative atmosphere that pervades the area.


In addition to Oudong, Kandal is home to a number of other important temples, including Wat Phnom Thon Mond, known for its vibrant murals and sacred shrines, and Wat Kean Svay Krau, a riverside temple that is a popular spot for both worship and relaxation. The province’s temples are often centers of community life, where locals gather for religious ceremonies, festivals, and social events.


Kandal’s villages offer an authentic glimpse into traditional Cambodian life. Many of these villages are known for their handicrafts, such as silk weaving, pottery, and palm sugar production. Visitors can explore these crafts firsthand, meeting artisans who continue to practice techniques passed down through generations. The province is also famous for its floating villages along the Mekong River, where life is intimately connected to the water. Houses are built on stilts, and boats are the primary means of transportation, creating a unique way of life that has adapted to the rhythms of the river.


Despite its proximity to Phnom Penh, Kandal remains a peaceful haven where the pace of life slows down. The province offers a perfect blend of natural beauty, historical richness, and cultural depth, making it an ideal destination for those looking to explore Cambodia beyond the capital. Whether you’re trekking up the hills of Oudong, cruising along the Mekong, or simply enjoying the tranquility of a rural village, Kandal Province provides a profound and immersive experience that showcases the enduring spirit and heritage of Cambodia.

PLACE TO VISIT


Oudong Mountain, known locally as Phnom Oudong, is a site of immense historical and spiritual significance in Cambodia. Once the royal capital of the country from the early 17th century until the mid-19th century, Oudong is situated about 40 kilometers northwest of Phnom Penh, making it a convenient yet profoundly enriching day trip. The site is perched on a series of hills that rise majestically from the surrounding plains, offering stunning panoramic views that extend as far as the eye can see, particularly over the lush rice paddies and the distant silhouette of Phnom Penh. The climb to the top of Oudong involves a journey through history and spirituality. As you ascend the long, winding staircases, you pass by various stupas, some of which date back several centuries. These stupas, with their ornate carvings and towering spires, house the remains of former Khmer kings and sacred relics, including what is believed to be a relic of the Buddha himself. The architecture of these stupas reflects a blend of traditional Khmer design with influences from the post-Angkorian period, showcasing the evolution of Cambodian architectural styles. The most prominent structure at Oudong is the massive stupa known as Chedi Preah Ath Roes, which dominates the skyline and serves as a focal point for visitors and pilgrims alike. The stupa is intricately decorated with carvings and bas-reliefs that depict scenes from Buddhist scriptures, adding to the site's sacred atmosphere. At the summit, the views are breathtaking, with the Mekong River winding its way through the landscape, and on a clear day, the spires of Phnom Penh can be seen in the distance. The peacefulness of the site, combined with its historical depth, makes Oudong a place of reflection and reverence, where visitors can connect with Cambodia's royal past and spiritual heritage.

The Silk Island, locally known as Koh Dach, is a serene and picturesque island located in the middle of the Mekong River, just a short ferry ride from Phnom Penh. This island is a hidden gem, renowned for its traditional silk weaving, a craft that has been passed down through generations of local artisans. Koh Dach is not just a place to observe the intricate process of silk production; it is a vibrant community where the rhythms of daily life are deeply intertwined with this ancient craft. Upon arriving at Koh Dach, visitors are greeted by the tranquil atmosphere of the island, with its narrow lanes lined with lush greenery, wooden stilt houses, and the soft hum of looms in the distance. The island’s landscape is a blend of fertile fields, orchards, and small farms, providing a peaceful backdrop for exploration. The journey around the island is best experienced by bicycle, allowing you to slowly soak in the sights and sounds of rural Cambodia. The heart of Koh Dach lies in its family-run silk workshops, where you can witness the entire process of silk production, from the rearing of silkworms to the spinning, dyeing, and weaving of silk threads. Artisans demonstrate their skills on traditional wooden looms, creating stunning silk textiles that range from scarves and shawls to elaborate garments. Each piece of silk is crafted with meticulous care, showcasing the artisans’ dedication to preserving this centuries-old tradition. Visitors are often invited to try their hand at weaving, offering a hands-on experience of this intricate craft. Beyond its cultural significance, Koh Dach also offers natural beauty and recreational opportunities. The island’s sandy riverbanks are perfect for a leisurely picnic or a swim in the Mekong’s refreshing waters. The local market is another highlight, where you can purchase handmade silk products directly from the weavers, supporting the local economy and taking home a unique piece of Cambodian heritage. Koh Dach is an idyllic escape from the hustle and bustle of Phnom Penh, offering a rich cultural experience amidst the serene beauty of the Mekong River.

Phnom Prasith is located in Makak commune, Ang Snoul district and in Chhviang commune, Ponhea Leu district, north of Phnom Penh. To get there, take National Road 5 north about 11 kilometers to Prek Pneuv Market point. From there, turn left and drive another 13 kilometers along a dirt trail. The total distance is about 24 kilometers from Phnom Penh or about 35 kilometers from Takmao provincial town. In the future, once National Road 26 is completed and the trail to Phnom Prasith is improved, visitors will be able to visit Phnom Prasith, and then continue on to Phnom Udong. Or, they could visit Udong first and Phnom Prasith later. Phnom Prasith is composed of two hills, the East Hill and the West Hill. The West Hill has no stories. The East Hill, however, has stories relating to religion and the Khmer king. It also has a large statue of the reclining Buddha reaching nirvana.There are not many big trees in Phnom Prasith, and there are no development plans for now. The site attracts few visitors because there is poor organization and management. Nevertheless, this area has beautiful scenery, abundant fresh air and is rich in culture and history. About 1 kilometer from Phnom Prasith, there is a man-made cultural site called Phnom Reap or Prasat Vihear Suor. Based on the style of Angkor Wat which is why it is called the New Angkor Wat, Phnom Reap was constructed of stone and cement between 1996 and 1998 for about 1 million US dollars. The money was raised from local and foreign sources by Mr. Meas Sarin.Today, Phnom Reap attracts many Cambodian and foreign tourists. It is often used as the backdrop for Cambodian videos.
